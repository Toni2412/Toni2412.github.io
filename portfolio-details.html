THEN AND NUDE

  1. Home

This series of aquarelle nudes draws inspiration from both historical paintings and contemporary mirror selfies. It explores the contrast and parallels between today’s self-representation of the female body and the perspectives of the past.

In art history, the naked female body has often been depicted by male artists—elevated to a subject of beauty, idealization, and 'high art,' but also stripped of agency, reduced to objects of desire under the male gaze. These women were rarely given a voice of their own; they were muses, symbols, or fantasies—seen, but not seeing. Their nudity was aestheticized, made acceptable, and their depiction became central to Western ideas of beauty and femininity.

In contrast, women today who share images of their own naked bodies are often dismissed or condemned. They are labeled as vain, desperate, or 'slutty.' Their self-expression is seen as attention-seeking or morally questionable behavior. By placing these two forms of representation side by side, the work invites a re-evaluation of how we view, value, and judge the nude female body—depending on who creates it and in what context.

The paintings seek to put these representations on equal footing: the woman painted by a Renaissance master and the woman photographing herself in a bathroom mirror are both reflections of their time. The legacy of the past is far from gone. The ways women have been portrayed for centuries—idealized, perfected, posed for male consumption—still echo in contemporary self-imagery. In our present world, where self-representation is mediated by social media, filters, and inherited beauty standards, the line between agency and performance remains complex.

This series aims to question how we see the female body, how we see women, and how women have learned to see themselves.
